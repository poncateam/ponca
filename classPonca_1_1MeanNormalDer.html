Compute the derivative of the mean normal vector of the input points.

Step-by-step derivation of the mean normal :

Given the definition of the mean normal \( n(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\sum_i w_\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{n_i}) \mathbf{n_i}}{\sum_i w_\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{n_i})} \), where \(\left[\mathbf{n_i} \in \text{neighborhood}(\mathbf{x})\right]\) are all the normal samples in \(\mathbf{x}\)'s neighborhood.

We denote \( t(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i w_\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{n_i}) \mathbf{n_i} \) and \( s(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i w_\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{n_i})\), such that \( n(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{t(\mathbf{x})}{s(\mathbf{x})}\).

By definition, \( n'(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{s(\mathbf{x})t'(\mathbf{x}) - t(\mathbf{x})s'(\mathbf{x})}{s(\mathbf{x})^2}\).

Assuming the weight of each normal is dependent on the position, we have \( s'(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i w'_\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{n_i}) \).

We rewrite \( t(\mathbf{x}) = \sum u(\mathbf{x})v(\mathbf{x}) \), with \( u(\mathbf{x}) = w_\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{n_i}) \) and \( v(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{n_i} \).

Assuming the normal vectors themselves do not change with position, \(v(\mathbf{x})\) is constant, its derivative is null, and so \( t'(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i u'(\mathbf{x}) v(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i w'_\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{n_i}) \mathbf{n_i} \).

Which leads to \( n'(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\sum_i w'\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{n_i}) \mathbf{n_i} - n(\mathbf{x})\sum w'(\mathbf{x})}{\sum_i w\mathbf{x}(\mathbf{n_i})} \).
